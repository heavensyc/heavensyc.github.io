This is not true. Let's say you have the moon and a spaceship orbiting the earth, in the same orbit. The earth's gravity will exert the same amount of pull on both the moon and the spaceship. HOWEVER, you're missing the fact that the objects orbiting also exerts a pull on earth, which means the moon would have to move faster than the spaceship to maintain the same orbit. To prove this, let's look at the formula for orbital speed, which says that v = √((m1+m2)*G)/r, where r is the distance between the center of the earth and the object, G is the gravitational constant (6.674×10−11), and m1 and m2 are the masses of earth and the object orbiting. Intuetively looking at the formula, and you'll see that the spaceship doesn't need to move as fast as the moon to stay in the same orbit, because less mass is inputted into the formula.
